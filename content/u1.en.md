## Core Notes

### One chip, a whole computer

At the heart of the Arduino Uno is an **ATmega328P** microcontroller — it packs a CPU, memory, timers, an ADC and a range of communication peripherals into a single chip. That's what "microcontroller" means: not a computer that needs bolted-on memory and a disk, but a self-contained little computer.

### The pinout: first learn what each pin can do

The Uno's pins fall into three groups:

- **Digital pins 0–13:** digital input or output. Of these, **3, 5, 6, 9, 10, 11** (marked `~`) support PWM. Pins 0 and 1 are also the serial RX/TX used when uploading, so avoid tying them up.
- **Analog pins A0–A5:** wired to the ADC; use `analogRead` for an analog voltage (0–1023). When unused they also work as ordinary digital pins.
- **Power pins:** `5V`, `3.3V`, `GND`, `Vin` — for powering peripherals and sharing ground.

Function multiplexing at a glance:

| Function | Pins |
|---|---|
| PWM (`~`) | 3, 5, 6, 9, 10, 11 |
| External interrupt | 2, 3 |
| UART serial | 0 (RX), 1 (TX) |
| I²C | A4 (SDA), A5 (SCL) |
| SPI | 10 (SS), 11 (MOSI), 12 (MISO), 13 (SCK) |
| Onboard LED | 13 |

### Power and 5V logic

The Uno runs from USB (5V) or from `Vin` at 7–12V through its onboard regulator down to 5V. It is **5V logic**: HIGH ≈ 5V. Be careful when connecting 3.3V parts (many sensors, ESP modules) — 5V straight into a 3.3V pin can damage it, so you need level shifting or a divider (see CF3).

Per-pin output current is limited (~20–40 mA), and the whole chip has a total cap. High-current loads like motors or LED strips must have an external driver (see AD2).

### What each of the three memories does

The MCU splits storage into three parts; understanding them heads off a lot of "my variable vanished" and "my program won't fit" confusion:

| Memory | Size | Traits | Holds |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Flash** | 32 KB | non-volatile, mostly read-only | your program (sketch) |
| **SRAM** | 2 KB | volatile, read/write, fast | run-time variables |
| **EEPROM** | 1 KB | non-volatile, read/write, slow | settings to keep long-term |

> ⚠ **Key point:** SRAM is only 2 KB and precious. Big arrays and long strings can exhaust it and cause bizarre crashes. Keep constant strings in Flash with `F("...")`. Giving every variable a "home" in your head is one of the biggest mindset shifts from PC programming to embedded.
