## Core Notes

### A capacitor: a little tank that stores charge

A capacitor is two conductive plates with an insulator between them. Apply a voltage and positive/negative charge piles up on the two plates — it **stores charge**. How much is set by the capacitance C (unit **farad, F**; in practice µF, nF, pF):

> **Q = C · V** (charge = capacitance × voltage)

Back to the water metaphor: a capacitor is a small tank. Voltage is the water level, and capacitance is the tank's cross-section. A bigger capacitor stores more charge and its voltage changes more *slowly*.

### RC: resistor + capacitor = time

A bare capacitor charges instantly. But put a resistor in series to limit the charging current and the system gains **time**. That pace is captured by the **time constant τ (tau)**:

> **τ = R · C** (in seconds)

While charging, the capacitor voltage approaches the supply Vs along an exponential curve:

```text
V(t) = Vs · (1 − e^(−t/τ))
```

Remember three intuitions:

| Time | Charged to |
|---|---|
| 1 τ | ~63% |
| 3 τ | ~95% |
| 5 τ | ~99% (call it full) |

Discharge is the mirror image, `V(t) = V0 · e^(−t/τ)`, again essentially done after 5τ. In the interactive figure, raise R or C and the curve flattens, taking longer to fill — that's the basis of using RC as a **delay**.

### Decoupling capacitors: a tiny reservoir for digital circuits

A chip switching suddenly demands a burst of current, which momentarily drops the supply voltage (ripple). Placing a small capacitor (typically 0.1 µF) right between each chip's power and ground pins gives it a reservoir on its doorstep: when the chip needs current it takes it from there first, so the voltage doesn't collapse. This is a **decoupling / bypass capacitor**.

> Rule of thumb: one 0.1 µF ceramic capacitor per digital chip, as close to the power pin as possible. It's the cheapest, most effective noise measure there is.

### First look at an RC low-pass filter

The same RC structure also works as a **low-pass filter**: input through the resistor, output across the capacitor. Fast-changing signals (high-frequency noise) can't charge/discharge the capacitor in time and get smoothed away; slow (low-frequency) signals pass. Smoothing a PWM signal with RC into an approximate DC voltage is exactly this idea (see IO3).

> ✦ **Key point:** capacitors store charge, resistors limit current, and together they introduce *time*. Decoupling, delays, filtering, debouncing — the many time-related phenomena in embedded work all trace back to τ = RC.
