## Core Notes

### Three quantities: voltage, current, resistance

To understand hardware, first build a reliable mental picture — the **water-pipe model**. Imagine a stretch of circuit as a pipe:

- **Voltage (V)** is like water pressure. It's the potential-energy difference that *pushes* charge along, measured in **volts (V)**. No pressure difference, no flow; no voltage difference, no current.
- **Current (I)** is like the flow rate — how much charge passes a cross-section each second, measured in **amperes (A)**. One amp is a lot; in embedded work you'll more often see milliamps (mA, thousandths of an amp).
- **Resistance (R)** is like the pipe's narrowness or a valve. The narrower the pipe, the harder water flows; the larger the resistance, the smaller the current. Measured in **ohms (Ω)**.

### Ohm's law: V = I · R

The three quantities aren't independent — **Ohm's law** binds them together:

> Voltage = current × resistance, i.e. **V = I · R**

From it you can rearrange to whichever unknown you need:

| To find | Formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Current | `I = V / R` | 5V across 220Ω → I = 5/220 ≈ 22.7 mA |
| Resistance | `R = V / I` | Want only 10 mA from 12V → R = 12/0.01 = 1200 Ω |
| Voltage | `V = I · R` | 10 mA through 470Ω → V = 0.01×470 = 4.7 V |

**Memory trick:** put V at the top of a triangle, I and R in the two bottom cells. Cover the quantity you want; the layout of the other two is the formula (V over ⇒ divide; I·R side by side ⇒ multiply).

### Series and parallel

- **Series (end to end):** current is the same everywhere; resistances add. `R = R1 + R2 + …`. Voltage dividers rely on this (see CF3).
- **Parallel (across the same two nodes):** voltage is the same across each; resistance is the reciprocal of the sum of reciprocals. `1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + …`. Two equal resistors in parallel halve the total.

### Power: circuits get warm

Current through a resistance does work and generates heat — that's **power**, in **watts (W)**:

> **P = V · I = I²·R = V²/R**

These three equivalent forms are invaluable when picking parts. A 220Ω resistor with 5V across it: P = 5²/220 ≈ 0.11 W, well within an ordinary 1/4 W (0.25 W) resistor. But put 12V straight across 100Ω: P = 144/100 = 1.44 W — an ordinary resistor would get hot and could burn out; you'd need a higher-wattage part.

```text
Choosing a resistor isn't only about ohms (Ω);
it's also about watts (W). Both must be adequate for it to be safe.
```

> ⚠ **Common misconception:** current isn't "forced" into a load by the supply — it's set jointly by voltage and resistance. The same 5V supply pushes 23 mA through 220Ω but tries to push 500 mA through 10Ω. That's why a direct short is dangerous: R→0, I→huge.
