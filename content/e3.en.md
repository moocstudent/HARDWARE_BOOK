## Core Notes

### Slicing a voltage with two resistors

Often you have 5V but want 3.3V, or a voltage that tracks the light level. The simplest tool is a **voltage divider**: two resistors in series between the supply and ground, with the output tapped from the middle.

Call the upper resistor R1 (to Vin) and the lower R2 (to GND); the tap between them is the output:

> **Vout = Vin · R2 / (R1 + R2)**

The derivation is direct: in a series circuit the current is the same, I = Vin/(R1+R2); Vout is the drop across R2 = I·R2. Substitute to get the formula.

| R1 | R2 | Vin | Vout |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1kΩ | 1kΩ | 5V | 2.50 V (half) |
| 2kΩ | 1kΩ | 5V | 1.67 V |
| 1kΩ | 2kΩ | 5V | 3.33 V |
| 10kΩ | 20kΩ | 5V | 3.33 V (same ratio) |

Note: **it depends only on the ratio**. 1k/2k and 10k/20k give the same Vout, but larger values draw less current and waste less power.

### A potentiometer is an adjustable divider

A **potentiometer** is a three-terminal part: the two ends connect to Vin and GND, and the sliding contact (wiper) splits the resistance into an upper and a lower part. Turning the knob changes the R1/R2 ratio, so the wiper outputs a voltage continuously adjustable from 0 to Vin — exactly the principle behind using it as a "knob".

### Sensor dividers: reading a resistance change as a voltage

An MCU's ADC reads voltage, not resistance. So sensors whose *resistance* changes with the environment — a **photoresistor (LDR)**, a **thermistor** — are paired with a fixed resistor to form a divider that turns the resistance change into a voltage change you can read:

```text
Vin ── LDR ──┬── to ADC pin
             │
        fixed resistor R
             │
            GND
```

More light → lower LDR resistance → less voltage across the LDR → the tap voltage rises. The fixed resistor's value sets where the output swings most over the light range you care about (a rule of thumb: pick it close to the sensor's mid-range resistance).

### Loading: don't power a circuit from a divider

A divider is **only** good for a reference voltage or a signal, not for powering a circuit. The moment you hang a current-drawing load off the tap (effectively another resistor in parallel with R2), Vout sags and the ratio is thrown off. Under load, either make R1 and R2 far smaller than the load, or use a proper regulator (LDO) instead.

> ✦ **Key point:** a divider is passive and non-amplifying. It can make a voltage *smaller*, but it can't produce more than Vin and can't supply much current. Its value is for *reading*, not *driving*.
