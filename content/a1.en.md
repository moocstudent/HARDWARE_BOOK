## Core Notes

### A servo: you just send an angle

An ordinary motor spins continuously once powered, and it's hard to stop it at a precise angle. A **servo** is different: inside it packs a DC motor, a gear train, a potentiometer that measures its angle, and a control circuit. Tell it "I want 90°" and it turns there itself and **holds** that position. This "send an angle, it goes there" ability comes from internal **closed-loop feedback**.

A common hobby servo (like the SG90) sweeps roughly 0°–180°.

### Pulse width is angle

A servo doesn't listen to a voltage but to the **width of a pulse**. It expects a pulse every **20 ms (50 Hz)**, and the duration the pulse stays HIGH sets the target angle:

| Pulse width | Angle |
|---|---|
| 1.0 ms | 0° |
| 1.5 ms | 90° (centre) |
| 2.0 ms | 180° |

> Widths from 1–2 ms map linearly to 0°–180°.

In the interactive figure, drag the angle and the HIGH portion of the pulse on the left widens while the horn on the right rotates.

### Don't confuse it with dimming PWM

An easy point of confusion: the servo signal is **also** a kind of PWM, but its concern differs from the LED-dimming PWM of IO3 —

- **Dimming PWM:** cares about **duty cycle** (the average); frequency matters less.
- **Servo PWM:** frequency is fixed at **50 Hz**, and what matters is the **absolute HIGH time** (1–2 ms), not the duty cycle.

Because of this, `analogWrite` can't control a servo precisely; use the dedicated `Servo` library, which generates the exact 1–2 ms pulses behind the scenes.

### Using the Servo library

```cpp
#include <Servo.h>
Servo myservo;

void setup() {
  myservo.attach(9);      // signal wire to pin 9
}
void loop() {
  myservo.write(0);    delay(1000);   // go to 0°
  myservo.write(90);   delay(1000);   // go to 90°
  myservo.write(180);  delay(1000);   // go to 180°
}
```

`write(angle)` takes 0–180 and the library converts it to a pulse width automatically.

A servo (three wires — brown/red/orange: GND / +5V / signal) draws real current when it moves, hundreds of milliamps at start-up. **Don't power it straight from the MCU's 5V pin**, or the voltage sags and the board may keep resetting. Instead, power the servo from a separate 5V supply — but **always tie its ground to the MCU's (share GND)**, or the signal has no common reference and the servo jitters.

> ⚠ **Key point:** a servo = motor + gears + feedback, and you send an *angle*, not a speed. Its control signal is a fixed 50 Hz, 1–2 ms pulse width. Two iron rules: use the `Servo` library, and give it its own supply with a shared ground.
