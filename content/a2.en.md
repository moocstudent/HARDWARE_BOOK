## Core Notes

### Motors can't wire straight to a pin

A motor is the first "high-power" load in this course. Two traits mean it must never hang directly off an MCU pin like an LED:

1. **High current:** even a small motor's start-up current reaches hundreds of mA to amps, far past the pin limit (~20–40 mA).
2. **Back-EMF:** a motor is essentially a coil, and cutting the power makes it fling a reverse high-voltage spike — enough to punch through a fragile chip.

So a **driver** must sit between motor and MCU: a transistor/MOSFET for low currents, but more often a ready-made **driver board**.

### The H-bridge: the secret of direction

A DC motor reverses when you reverse the voltage. An **H-bridge** is a circuit of four switches (shaped like the letter H); by choosing which two close, you route current through the motor in either direction — **forward / reverse / brake**. Add PWM (see IO3) and you also get **speed control**.

Common driver chips L298N, DRV8833, etc. are all H-bridges at heart:

```cpp
// say IN1/IN2 control one motor channel
digitalWrite(IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(IN2, LOW);  // forward
digitalWrite(IN1, LOW);  digitalWrite(IN2, HIGH); // reverse
digitalWrite(IN1, LOW);  digitalWrite(IN2, LOW);  // stop
analogWrite(ENA, 180);   // PWM speed on the enable pin
```

### Flyback diode: an escape route for back-EMF

Driving an inductive load (motor, relay, solenoid), place a **flyback diode** reverse-parallel across the load so that at power-off the coil current bleeds safely through the diode instead of slamming the switch. Integrated driver boards usually include one.

### Stepper motors: precise, one step at a time

A stepper doesn't "just spin" — it **turns one step per pulse**, advancing a fixed small angle (e.g. 1.8°/step) for each pulse received. That lets it **position precisely without feedback**, common in 3D printers and CNC. It energises several internal coils **in sequence** to create a rotating field that "drags" the rotor along, driven through dedicated boards like the ULN2003 or A4988, with the MCU sending "step + direction" signals.

> ⚠ **Key point:** a motor = high current + back-EMF, always connected through a **driver**. Run a DC motor's direction with an **H-bridge** and its speed with **PWM**, protected by a **flyback diode**; position a stepper with **stepped pulses**. Power the motor separately and **share ground** with the MCU.
